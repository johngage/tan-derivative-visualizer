Step-by-step geometric proof

Scroll through the derivation

Each step activates a stage of the proof on the diagram. The SVG updates as you read — no interaction required.

Step 01

Set up the unit circle and tangent line

Draw a unit circle centred at the origin O. A point P = (cos θ, sin θ) sits on the circle, reached by a radial ray at angle θ. The tangent line is the vertical line x = 1 — it is tangent to the circle at (1, 0).

Where the ray OP, when extended, meets the line x = 1, the height of that intersection above the x-axis is exactly tan(θ) = sin(θ)/cos(θ). This is the geometric definition of tangent.

tan(θ) = height on x = 1 = sin(θ) / cos(θ)
Step 02

Add a second ray at θ + Δθ

Advance the angle by a small increment Δθ, creating a second ray at angle θ + Δθ. This ray meets x = 1 at height tan(θ + Δθ).

The vertical gap between the two intersection points on the tangent line is Δtan = tan(θ + Δθ) − tan(θ). We want to understand this gap geometrically as Δθ → 0.

Δtan = tan(θ+Δθ) − tan(θ)
Step 03

The arc PQ on the unit circle

Points P and Q are the tips of the two radial rays on the unit circle. The arc between them has length equal to the angle in radians: |arc PQ| = Δθ.

When Δθ is small, the chord PQ ≈ arc PQ ≈ Δθ, and the chord is nearly perpendicular to the radius OP at P. This perpendicularity is exact in the limit Δθ → 0 — it is the geometric fact that a tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius.

|chord PQ| ≈ Δθ (radians) as Δθ → 0
Step 04

Similar triangles connect arc to Δtan

Here is Needham's key insight. The small triangle at P (formed by the chord PQ, the radius OP, and a perpendicular) is geometrically similar to the large right triangle formed by:

the origin O, the foot of the tangent line (1, 0), and the point (1, tan θ). That large triangle has base = 1, height = tan(θ), and hypotenuse = sec(θ) = 1/cos(θ).

By the similarity ratio, the Δtan increment relates to the arc Δθ via the square of the secant:

Δtan / Δθ ≈ sec(θ) × sec(θ) = sec²(θ)
Step 05

Take the limit — Δθ → 0

As Δθ → 0, the chord-to-arc ratio → 1, the perpendicularity of PQ to OP becomes exact, and the similar-triangle approximation becomes an equality. The ratio Δtan/Δθ converges to sec²(θ).

Finally, the Pythagorean identity sec²(θ) = 1 + tan²(θ) gives us the result in its most beautiful form — the derivative of tan(θ) expressed entirely in terms of tan(θ) itself:

d/dθ [tan θ] = sec²(θ) = 1 + tan²(θ)

Why it's geometrically elegant

d/dθ [ tan θ ] = sec²(θ) = 1 + tan²(θ)

The derivative of tan(θ) is expressed entirely in terms of tan(θ) itself. No limits computed analytically — only similar triangles, the definition of arc length, and one Pythagorean identity.

01
Unit circle definition
tan(θ) is the height on the vertical tangent line x = 1 where the radial ray meets it.
02
Arc length = Δθ (rad)
On a unit circle, arc length equals the subtended angle in radians. This is why radians are natural.
03
Similar triangles
The tiny chord triangle at P is similar to the large secant triangle — the similarity ratio is sec(θ).
04
Pythagorean identity
sec²(θ) = 1 + tan²(θ) reveals that the derivative is a quadratic in the function itself.
