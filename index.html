Geometric Proof
1
Setup: unit circle & tangent line
Draw a unit circle (radius = 1). The point P = (cos θ, sin θ) sits on the circle. The tangent line is the vertical line x = 1. Its height — where the radial ray at angle θ meets x = 1 — is exactly tan(θ).
tan(θ) = sin(θ) / cos(θ) = height on tangent line
2
Add Δθ: a second ray
Advance to angle θ + Δθ. The new ray meets x = 1 at height tan(θ + Δθ). The change in height is Δtan = tan(θ + Δθ) − tan(θ).
tan(θ) → tan(θ+Δθ)
Δtan = difference in heights
3
Key triangle on the circle
The arc PQ between the two points on the unit circle has length ≈ Δθ (in radians) when Δθ is small. The chord PQ is nearly perpendicular to the radius at P. This is the infinitesimal right triangle at the heart of the proof.
|arc PQ| ≈ Δθ (radians)
chord ⊥ radius (limit)
4
Similar triangles link arc to Δtan
The small triangle at P on the circle is similar to the large right triangle formed by the origin, the foot of the tangent, and the top of the tan segment. The large triangle has hypotenuse = 1/cos(θ) = sec(θ), so by similarity:
Δtan / Δθ ≈ sec(θ) × sec(θ)
        = sec²(θ)
5
Take the limit: Δθ → 0
As Δθ → 0, the similar-triangle approximation becomes exact. The ratio Δtan / Δθ converges to sec²(θ). Since sec²(θ) = 1 + tan²(θ) (Pythagorean identity), we get the beautiful geometric result:
d/dθ [tan θ] = sec²(θ)
              = 1 + tan²(θ)
